TITLE: Tenant and Landlord Rights (Rental Basics)

SECTION 1: The Rent Agreement
A rent or lease agreement is a contract between a landlord (lessor) and tenant (lessee) setting out the terms of occupation, including rent amount, security deposit, duration, and conditions for renewal or termination. Oral rental agreements are legally valid in many cases, but a written and registered agreement is strongly advisable since it is far easier to enforce and prove in a dispute.

SECTION 2: Security Deposit
A security deposit is a sum paid by the tenant to the landlord to cover potential damages or unpaid dues, refundable at the end of the tenancy after deducting any legitimate dues such as unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear and tear. Excessive or unreasonable deposits, and unreasonable delay or refusal to refund, can be challenged.

SECTION 3: Landlord's Right to Evict
A landlord generally cannot evict a tenant arbitrarily or without following due process, even after the agreement period ends, unless the rental agreement or applicable law specifically provides for it. Common lawful grounds for eviction include non-payment of rent, unauthorized subletting, use of the premises for illegal purposes, or the landlord's genuine need to reoccupy the property (bona fide requirement), and typically require proper notice and, if contested, a court or rent authority order.

SECTION 4: Tenant's Right to Essential Services
A tenant has the right to continued access to essential services such as water and electricity during the tenancy. A landlord cutting off essential supplies to force a tenant out, instead of following the lawful eviction process, is generally considered an unlawful act and can be legally challenged.

SECTION 5: Maintenance and Repairs
Responsibility for repairs is typically divided by the nature of the issue: landlords are usually responsible for structural repairs and major maintenance, while tenants are generally responsible for minor day-to-day upkeep, unless the rental agreement states otherwise.

SECTION 6: Rent Increase
A landlord generally cannot increase rent arbitrarily mid-term without a clause in the agreement permitting it. Rent increases are typically only enforceable at the time of renewal, and any hike should be reasonable and, where applicable, in line with local rent control provisions.
